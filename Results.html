Diverging Trends of NO₂ and PM₂.₅: A Decadal National Overview

From the 10-year analysis of both pollutants, it can be seen that their trends are fairly consistent, with NO₂ almost always remaining at low concentrations across the entire country, while PM₂.₅ maintains high concentrations over nearly the entire national territory.

As shown on the maps, there is only one area where both pollutants overlap almost perfectly, which corresponds to the capital. The analysis of the graphs further highlights the difference between the two pollutants, as NO₂ never exceeds the EU average and always remains below the limits set by the WHO. PM₂.₅, on the other hand, although its annual average remains below European limits, has in recent years repeatedly recorded exceedances of the standards.











Seasonal Dynamics of NO₂ and PM₂.₅ Concentrations

From the four images on the right, it can be observed that, due to its low levels, NO₂ does not undergo significant seasonal variation throughout the year.

The maps clearly show a high concentration around the capital area and low concentrations in the north of the country, while along the entire mountain range, the levels remain very low.

The situation is quite different for PM₂.₅, as it clearly undergoes significant variation between the winter and summer seasons.

Although, in general, pollution levels across the country are higher compared to the previous pollutant, there is a clear increase in concentrations during the winter period, likely due to heating systems. This change is particularly noticeable in the northern areas of the country.

Urban Hotspots: Correlating Air Pollution with Population Density

Knowing that high levels of pollutants can harm people’s health, in the maps on the left we linked pollutant concentrations to urban areas in order to see whether a correlation exists.

Although NO₂ concentrations are generally low, they are higher around the capital—the most urbanized area—compared to the rest of the country.

The situation is certainly more concerning when it comes to PM₂.₅. In this case, the concentration levels in all major urban centers across the country are critical, and the problem is not limited to the capital but also affects other urban areas. Furthermore, unlike NO₂, PM₂.₅ concentrations remain very high across the entire territory. This makes PM₂.₅ the most dangerous pollutant in this area.

According to the Current WHO limit values, the Annual Limit Values are:

NO₂: 10 µg/m³
PM₂.₅: 5 µg/m³
According to the Current EU limit values, the Annual Limit Values are:

NO₂: 40 µg/m³
PM₂.₅: 25 µg/m³

Air Pollution and Mortality: A Comparative Analysis of NO₂ and PM₂.₅

To confirm what was stated in the previous paragraph, the two maps on the right highlight the significant differences between the two pollutants.

The first map shows the mortality rate in Europe due to NO₂. As can be seen, Bosnia and Herzegovina does not have one of the highest rates in Europe, recording 151 deaths per 100,000 caused by NO₂ pollution.

A completely different and consistent situation with the previously discussed data is shown in the map on the right. As seen, Bosnia and Herzegovina is one of the European countries with the highest mortality rate linked to PM₂.₅. In 2022, there were as many as 2,437 deaths per 100,000 due to this pollutant.

Conclusions

Conclusions

In conclusion, we can say that in recent years Bosnia is facing an increasingly serious problem with the rise of PM₂.₅ pollution. In particular, over the last 5 years, the concentration of this pollutant in the air has grown rapidly and will present Bosnia and Herzegovina with new and significant environmental challenges.

The transition to more sustainable energy sources for vehicles and heating systems therefore becomes a priority to reduce this type of pollutant.
